What I Read

August 1, 2020

These are all the books I have ever read and what I think about them. The authors or books are in no particular order.

  1. Kafka, Franz:

    • The Trial : Read it when I was younger. Didn’t get the whole point across then but it slowly crept on. Intriguing writing. Fantastic scenes.
    • Metamorphosis : A short read that is just perfect. I still go through pages of this book from time to time. One of the greatest.

  2. J. R. R. Tolkien:

    • The Hobbit : A mesmerizing tale, nothing falls short. Wildly beautiful characters. Always recommended.
    • The Lord Of The Rings : Expands on the hobbit and does not cut any corners. One of the most beautiful tales possibly ever told.

  3. Gabriel Garcia Marquez:

    • One Hundred Years Of Solitude: One of the boldest and most timeless stories I have ever read. I would like to have been in Macondo at the time. Not many books have made me feel such a way.

  4. Huxley, Aldous:

    • Brave New World : Found it greately overrated. Didn’t like anything about it. Even the dystopian parts felt lazy. Island is a better work.
    • Island : Builds up great. Comes to a Huxley like closing. Like it better than BNW. It’s alright.
    • Doors of Perception : A fascinating account. Nonetheless, I recommended taking psilocybin instead of reading about it.

  5. Wagle, Narayan:

    • Palpasa Cafe : Overrated gargoyle. Reads like a real shitty Nepali movie. That’s not saying much.

  6. Thapa, Manjushree:

    • Forget Kathmandu : I want to forget that I read this book. Gives absolutely nothing, takes a few hours away. A cheap account of an Author’s trek.

  7. Sherchan, Bhupi:

    • Ghumne Mech Mathi Andho Manche : Must be one of the only real poets of this dammned nation. Of the few Nepali things that have moved me, this is one of them.

  8. Brown, Dan:

    • Da vinci Code : Read it a long long time ago. Found it amusing then, find it amusing now.
    • The Lost Symbol : Kek. Don’t remember much about it.

  9. Sir Arthur Conan Doyle:

    • The White Company : It’s nice and alright. Read it a long long time ago.

  10. Dostoevsky, Fyodor:

    • Crime and Punishment : Drags on and off. Gets very intense and introspective at parts. A rewarding read.

  11. Vonnegut, Kurt:

    • Slaughterhouse Five : A good one. Intriguing. Humorous.
    • Cat’s cradle : Liked this one. But have forgotten much about it.

  12. Ludwig J. Stiller:

    • Nepal: Growth of a Nation : I recommend this. I can’t compare it to much since I haven’t read much history.

  13. Sudheer Sharma:

    • Prayogshala : I always wondered what this book was supposed to be. A supplement of modern Nepali history? Secrets in bureaucracy? Didn’t even get around to finishing it.

  14. Salinger, J. D:

    • Catcher in the rye : A book worth remembering. I wanted to relate to the guy but all I could do was sympathize with him.
    • A Perfect Day for Bananafish : Search it and read this story. Nabokov highly recommended this one and I do too.

  15. Plato:

    • Alcibiades-I : Reading any philosophy is a mindfuck for the first time. I get this one. Other dialogues I am slowly getting.

  16. Orwell, George:

    • Animal Farm : If you want to start reading. Start here.

  17. Hemingway, Ernest:

    • The Old man and the Sea : One of the greatest stories I have ever read. Possibly my favorite story of all time.

I plan on reading more this year. Will update this list as time goes on. Got the idea from here.
